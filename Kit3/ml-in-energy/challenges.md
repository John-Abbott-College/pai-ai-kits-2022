## **Sustainability Challenges**

<table data-header-hidden><thead><tr><th width="171"></th><th></th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Say</strong></td><td><p>While ML has the potential to optimize energy production, distribution, and consumption, it also comes with certain ethical and environmental concerns [<a href="https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fenrg.2023.1071291/full">2</a>], [<a href="https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9883030">30</a>], [<a href="https://www.energyrev.org.uk/media/1985/energyrev_aiandethics_final_202207.pdf">31</a>].</p><p></p><p>For example, ML models, particularly deep learning models, require significant computational resources during training and operation [<a href="https://futurium.ec.europa.eu/en/european-ai-alliance/blog/sustainable-artificial-intelligence-energy-sector?language=es">32</a>]. This translates into high energy consumption, especially when training complex models over extended periods [<a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.11738">33</a>]. The energy consumed by ML systems, including the power needed to run GPUs and TPUs, can result in a substantial carbon footprint [<a href="https://futurium.ec.europa.eu/en/european-ai-alliance/blog/sustainable-artificial-intelligence-energy-sector?language=es">32</a>], [<a href="https://www.techtarget.com/searchenterpriseai/feature/AI-and-climate-change-The-mixed-impact-of-machine-learning">34</a>]. The energy demand for ML training can contribute to increased carbon emissions and strain on existing energy infrastructure [<a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.11738">33</a>].</p><p> </p><p>If you want to learn more about the sustainability issues related to the integration of ML in the energy sector, check out the learn more content.</p></td></tr><tr><td><strong>Ask</strong></td><td>What sustainability issues do you think smart homes may have?</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Do</strong></td><td>Give students 5 minutes to share their responses.</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Say</strong></td><td><p><em>[Depending on students' responses to the previous question, use the following information to fill in any knowledge gaps]</em></p><p> </p><p>While smart homes offer various benefits, they also pose certain sustainability challenges, such as:</p><ul><li><strong>Electronic Waste:</strong> Smart home devices often have a relatively short lifespan compared to traditional appliances [<a href="https://www.bitdefender.com/blog/hotforsecurity/smart-household-appliance-might-short-lifespan/">35</a>]. As technology advances, newer devices with additional features become available, prompting homeowners to upgrade their existing devices [<a href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/343436758_Towards_Longevity_of_Smart_Home_Systems">36</a>], [<a href="https://nymag.com/intelligencer/smarthome/very-happy-article-about-smart-homes-waste-and-obsolescence.html">37</a>]. This leads to an increase in electronic waste, as the discarded devices contribute to landfill pollution and the depletion of natural resources [<a href="https://pirg.org/articles/bricked-house-how-obsolescence-looms-over-our-smart-home-devices/">38</a>].</li><li> <strong>Energy Consumption:</strong> While smart homes can optimize energy usage through automation and intelligent control systems, the proliferation of multiple connected devices can lead to increased overall energy consumption [<a href="https://www.ijert.org/the-impact-of-smart-homes-on-energy-consumptions-a-survey">39</a>]. Each device, even in standby mode, consumes a certain amount of energy [<a href="https://www.ijert.org/the-impact-of-smart-homes-on-energy-consumptions-a-survey">39</a>], [<a href="https://www.the-ambient.com/features/smart-home-energy-use-costs-bills-2778">40</a>]. Additionally, the energy required for data transmission and processing in smart homes can contribute to the overall energy footprint [<a href="https://www.ijert.org/the-impact-of-smart-homes-on-energy-consumptions-a-survey">39</a>], [<a href="https://www.the-ambient.com/features/smart-home-energy-use-costs-bills-2778">40</a>].</li><li><strong>Manufacturing and Supply Chain:</strong> The production and distribution of smart home devices involve resource-intensive processes [<a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S030142151630711X">41</a>]. Additionally, the disposal of hazardous materials used in electronic devices, such as batteries, can pose environmental risks if not handled properly [<a href="https://www.itworldcanada.com/article/e-waste-techs-big-dirty-secret/481142">43</a>], [<a href="https://www.zdnet.com/article/our-old-devices-are-creating-a-mountain-of-e-waste-and-its-getting-a-lot-bigger/">44</a>].</li><li><strong>Data Centers and Cloud Services:</strong> Smart homes often rely on cloud-based services and data centers for data storage, processing, and analysis [<a href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/343436758_Towards_Longevity_of_Smart_Home_Systems">36</a>]. These data centers require significant amounts of energy to operate and cool the servers, resulting in carbon emissions and a strain on energy resources [<a href="https://www.datacenterknowledge.com/industry-perspectives/data-center-costs-driving-force-energy-efficiency-part-1">45</a>], [<a href="https://www.grcooling.com/blog/the-effects-of-data-centers-on-the-environment/">46</a>]. The sustainability of the infrastructure supporting smart home services should be considered [<a href="https://www.datacenterknowledge.com/industry-perspectives/data-center-costs-driving-force-energy-efficiency-part-1">45</a>].</li></ul></td></tr></tbody></table>
